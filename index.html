NOT ALL CHIPS
ARE EQUAL

The Energy and Access Politics of AI Hardware

Rishit Dwivedi — ENGL 2500, Iowa State University, March 2026

The processors running artificial intelligence aren't neutral — they shape who gets to build AI, how much energy it burns, and who pays the price.

"The chip you use determines the cost you pay — in watts, in dollars, and in access."
RQ1

Which hardware accelerators are most energy-efficient for deep learning workloads?

RQ2

Does increasingly specialized AI hardware primarily advantage large technology companies over smaller research institutions?

UNDER THE HOOD

A car's fuel economy is measured in miles per gallon; the energy efficiency of a processor is measured as tera operations per second per watt (TOPS/W). The term "tera" means 1 trillion; an "operation" is a fundamental mathematical calculation. A higher TOPS/W means the chip does more useful work with less energy. The three dominant AI accelerators differ dramatically across this metric — and those differences aren't just technical.

GPU, TPU, and FPGA compared side by side

GPU — Graphics Processing Unit

  • Originally built for video game graphics; repurposed for AI
  • General-purpose: handles video, crypto, neural networks
  • 5,120 CUDA cores; ~250W per chip
  • Open market — anyone can buy one
  • De facto standard for AI research due to accessibility
  • Less energy-efficient than TPU for AI-specific tasks
Best for: large-scale training HIGH ENERGY USE
Google Cloud Only

TPU — Tensor Processing Unit

  • Built by Google specifically for matrix multiplication
  • Systolic array design: data flows cell to cell without writing back to memory
  • TPU v4: 2.1× faster than TPU v3, 2.7× better performance per watt
  • Outperforms NVIDIA V100 by 1.1×–2.7× depending on task
  • Available only through Google Cloud — cannot be purchased
  • Google controls both hardware AND TensorFlow software stack
Best for: AI-specific workloads MOST EFFICIENT

FPGA — Field-Programmable Gate Array

  • Reprogrammable at the hardware level for any specific task
  • Removes wasted power from unused circuits
  • 1.39× more energy-efficient than GPU for edge inference
  • 4.67× more efficient than CPU
  • Railway inspection: 60 images/sec, <17ms latency
  • Requires specialized hardware-design expertise to program
Best for: inference at the edge SPECIALIZED EFFICIENT

No single accelerator is universally superior. The "best" chip depends entirely on the specific task being performed.

For translation tasks, TPUs outperform the NVIDIA V100 by 1.1×–1.7×. For image-recognition workloads — convolutional neural networks — that advantage widens to 1.5×–2.7×. The gap is not constant; it is task-dependent. This means that comparing chips in the abstract obscures the real question: what are they being asked to do, and who gets to ask it?

HARDWARE IS POLITICS

WHAT GETS BUILT

Since Google, Microsoft, and Meta are the leaders in building large-scale AI systems, these systems focus on their business models: ads, content recommendation, consumer services. The problems that command the most investment are the ones that generate the most revenue. Climate change modeling, indigenous language preservation, rural healthcare diagnostics — these are not ignored because they are unimportant. They are deferred because the entities who control the hardware are not accountable to those communities.

THE NAIRR PROBLEM

Proposed ways to "democratize" AI through programs like the National AI Research Resource (NAIRR) have relied solely on licenses from Big Tech companies that have control over all aspects of the underlying computing infrastructure — rather than working to create independent third-party resources. This means that even public programs designed to broaden access are structurally dependent on the same corporations that created the access gap in the first place. Access granted through a license is access that can be revoked.

GPUs democratized early AI research by making programmable parallel compute available on the open market. TPUs pushed the frontier of efficiency but placed it behind a proprietary wall. FPGAs opened a path to greener, task-specific AI that remains underutilized — partly because the expertise required to program them is itself unevenly distributed.

The GPU origin story — hardware built for video games, accidentally powering a revolution — captures something important: the infrastructure of AI was never designed for the purpose it now serves. Each generation of accelerator has been shaped by the economic interests of whoever funded its development.

When the most capable AI hardware is accessible only to Google, Microsoft, and a handful of other giants, the questions of who builds AI, whose problems it solves, and whose values it encodes are answered by default — not by choice. Hardware is not a neutral technical detail. It is the foundation upon which the entire AI ecosystem is built, and right now, that foundation is unequal.

HARDWARE IS POLICY.

The decisions made about which chips get built, sold, and shared are not technical decisions. They are political ones.
